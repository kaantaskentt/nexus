Nexus interview protocol

Section 7. Imminent harm and adverse events

What the capture agent does when a worker discloses harm, danger, or illegal conduct during a live interview.

Status: draft for review    Owner: Emre    Scope: all live Nexus capture interviews, all jurisdictions

7.1  Purpose and the one inversion

Every other section of this protocol tells the agent to capture neutrally and never resolve. Section 7 is the single exception. The moment a worker discloses harm, danger, or a crime, the agent stops capturing on that thread. It does not probe, grade, investigate, or repeat the disclosure back. Probing for detail is the failure here, not the fix, because it manufactures data and can deepen the harm.

Design principle. The agent makes no legal or clinical judgment. Nexus operates internationally, and reporting duties, privacy law, and duty-to-act rules differ in every jurisdiction and change over time. Rather than teach the agent to be correct in every legal system, we remove the legal decision from the agent entirely. The agent performs only the moves that are safe in every jurisdiction. A human reviewer, advised by counsel, makes the one hard call per case. A decision the agent never makes is a decision it can never make wrong.

7.2  What trips the protocol

Four kinds of disclosure move an interview out of normal capture and into this section. The agent recognizes the category coarsely and routes it. It does not rank severity itself; that is a human job (7.5).

Bucket

Examples

A. Danger to life

Active suicidal or self-harm intent. A threat of violence to another person. Ongoing abuse of a child or a vulnerable adult.

B. Ongoing harm to a person

Abuse or harassment where the worker is victim, witness, or perpetrator. A serious unsafe condition putting people at risk.

C. Illegal conduct

The client company's own illegality, such as fraud, wage theft, or safety and environmental violations. Or an individual worker's admission of a crime. These two cases are handled differently at escalation (7.7).

D. Nexus-caused adverse event

The interview itself distresses the worker, or a data or privacy incident occurs on Nexus's side.

7.3  The invariant core

These five moves are the same in every country. None of them depends on local law. They are the entire in-the-room protocol. Everything else in this section either supports them or governs what a human does afterward.

Detect. Recognize the signal and treat the thread as out of scope for capture.

Stop. Halt capture on that thread. Do not ask follow-up questions to establish detail.

Do not repeat. Do not restate the disclosure back, summarize it, or build on it.

Resource. If the disclosure involves personal danger, surface the region-appropriate safety resource packet.

Quarantine and notify. Segregate the record out of the knowledge base and notify the human reviewer.

Why this is the safest design. Liability comes from a discretionary decision taken in the wrong place. The agent takes none. Holding less data is the one protection that works identically everywhere, so the core minimizes what is kept. All variation by jurisdiction is pushed to a human plus counsel, and to a thin config (Appendix A), never into the agent.

7.4  The agent in the room

The agent is not a clinician, an investigator, or a reporter. Trained research interviewers ask follow-up questions to assess how acute a disclosure is. The Nexus agent must not. It has no clinical judgment to fall back on, and probing exposes it to confabulation and to sycophantic drift. So its job collapses to four moves: recognize, acknowledge without amplifying, serve the resource, hand off.

The never list, all tiers

Never promise confidentiality or secrecy.

Never give a legal or clinical opinion.

Never investigate or ask follow-up questions to establish detail.

Never grade, judge, or accuse.

Never express alarm that shames the worker.

Never contact third parties or authorities.

Never fold the disclosure into the knowledge base or a skill.

Reference phrasing

Illustrative, not a script to read verbatim. Tone is calm, brief, and non-judgmental.

Danger to life (Red). “Thank you for telling me. This matters, and it is outside what I am here to help with, so I want you to have the right support. [serve resource packet]. I am going to pause here and flag this to a person on the Nexus team. Please reach out to one of those resources or someone you trust.”

Serious harm or illegality (Amber). “Thanks for sharing that. It is not something I can take into the work we are capturing, and I will flag it for a person on our team to look at. We can keep going on the process side, or stop here. Your call.”

Sensitive but lower severity (Yellow). “Noted, thanks. Let us keep that separate from the workflow we are mapping. Back to the step you were describing.”

7.6  Data handling and minimization

Minimization is the master liability lever, because you cannot be fined or subpoenaed over sensitive information you did not keep. It works the same in every jurisdiction, which is why it sits in the invariant core.

Quarantine. Flagged disclosures are segregated from the client knowledge base and can never feed skill creation. A harm disclosure must never become captured process.

Minimize. Store only what the reviewer needs to act: category, tier, timestamp, session reference. Do not retain verbatim detail that raises liability without adding to the decision.

Segregate access. The incident record is access-controlled to the reviewer and named backup, not the general Nexus team and never the client.

Delete. Once the reviewer has acted, delete beyond the minimal incident log. Set a short retention limit.

Regulatory note. Both jurisdictions you operate in treat this content as high risk. Turkey's KVKK and the EU's GDPR both class criminal-offense and health data as special categories with heavy handling duties. The safe posture is not to secure this data well, it is not to hold it. Confirm the retention limit with counsel.

7.7  Escalation, roles, and the retaliation fork

The reviewer is the standing decision-maker, the equivalent of the safety monitor in a clinical study. Emre owns this role, given the clinical background, with a named backup. The reviewer assigns tier, consults the jurisdiction counsel note, and decides on any action. The agent notifies; the reviewer acts.

The retaliation fork. Standard escalation ladders send a concern upward to those charged with governance. That default is wrong for us in one case. When the disclosure is about the client company's own wrongdoing and the discloser is a worker, routing it to the client contact exposes the worker to retaliation. Rule: a disclosure of the client's own illegal or harmful conduct is never routed to the client contact. It goes only to the Nexus reviewer and, if needed, to counsel. Whether anything is reported onward is a counsel decision, handled away from the client's chain.

7.8  Consent and confidentiality

Nexus is a vendor, not a clinician, and holds no privilege. The protocol is only ethical if the limit is stated before the interview, not discovered during it. One line in onboarding consent, valid in every jurisdiction, converts a surprise escalation into a disclosed term:

If you disclose imminent harm or serious wrongdoing, we may stop the interview and escalate to a human reviewer, and we cannot guarantee confidentiality over that disclosure.

The agent never over-promises secrecy in the room, because the consent line already sets the honest expectation.

7.9  Legal posture and the counsel gate

Nexus positions itself as a data processor, not a reporter or investigator. The agent takes no reporting action, which avoids both breaching privacy by over-reporting and implying a duty of care Nexus cannot uniformly meet. Two supports carry the residual risk.

Contract. In the client DPA and MSA: fix Nexus as processor and the client as controller; require the client to hold lawful basis and to own any onward reporting; disclaim Nexus from investigation and reporting roles; and indemnify Nexus against the client's own illegal conduct. Risk transfer by contract is portable and does not depend on getting each statute right.

Counsel gate. The one place the invariant core can collide with local law is an affirmative duty to act, such as a general failure-to-report-a-crime offense. Whether such a duty reaches a software vendor is unsettled in some jurisdictions, including Turkey. This is resolved per case by the human reviewer on counsel's advice, never by the agent. Appendix A holds one counsel-maintained row per jurisdiction.
